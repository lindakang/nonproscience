Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a complex neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere with functioning or development. As of 2025, the understanding of ADHD has evolved from a simplistic “chemical imbalance” model to a sophisticated neurobiological framework involving multiple neurotransmitter systems and neural circuits.

As the most prevalent neurodevelopmental disorder, ADHD affects approximately 7.2% to 11% of children globally. Longitudinal data indicate that ADHD persists into adulthood in approximately 4% to 5% of the general population, representing roughly 60% of individuals diagnosed during childhood. Furthermore, emerging population-based cohort studies challenge the traditional “childhood-onset” requirement, suggesting that up to 20% of adult cases may first manifest clinically significant symptoms during late adolescence or young adulthood. This phenomenon has been described as “late-onset” ADHD.

Diagnosis and Symptoms

The clinical diagnosis of ADHD is based on criteria outlined in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5). The diagnosis requires a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity–impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. Symptoms must be present for at least six months, with an age of onset before 12 years. Additionally, symptoms must occur in more than one setting (e.g., home and school or work) and be developmentally and culturally inappropriate for the individual.

ADHD is phenotypically heterogeneous and is stratified into three primary clinical presentations, which guide treatment decisions:

  • Predominantly Inattentive Presentation (ADHD-I): Characterized by difficulty sustaining attention, poor organizational skills, and a tendency to abandon tasks. This presentation is frequently underdiagnosed because it lacks overt hyperactive or disruptive behaviours.
  • Predominantly Hyperactive/Impulsive Presentation (ADHD-H): Defined by excessive motor activity, fidgeting, difficulty remaining seated, and impulsive decision-making. This presentation is most commonly identified in early childhood.
  • Combined Presentation (ADHD-C): The most prevalent form of ADHD, meeting diagnostic criteria for both inattentive and hyperactive–impulsive symptom domains.

Mechanism of Action: Pathophysiology of ADHD

The core neurobiological dysfunction in ADHD is believed to involve dysregulation of catecholaminergic signalling—primarily dopamine (DA) and norepinephrine (NE), within executive control networks of the brain.

The Catecholamine Hypothesis

The prevailing hypothesis centers on the hypofunction of dopaminergic and noradrenergic systems within the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and basal ganglia (particularly the striatum).

  • Dopamine (DA): Dopamine modulates D1 receptor activity to reduce background “noise,” thereby enhancing focus on task-relevant stimuli. Insufficient dopamine signalling in the PFC reduces the signal-to-noise ratio, manifesting as impaired attention, working memory deficits, and reduced motivation. In the striatum, dopamine dysregulation contributes to impulsivity and motor hyperactivity.
  • Norepinephrine (NE): Norepinephrine enhances "signal" strength by increasing representational knowledge through the activation of the α2A receptor. It is critical for modulating cortical arousal and attention. Its deficiency in the PFC impairs the ability to filter out irrelevant stimuli and focus on goal-directed tasks.

Emerging Neurotransmitter Systems

Beyond catecholamines, additional neurotransmitter systems have been implicated in ADHD pathophysiology, opening avenues for novel therapeutic strategies:

  • Glutamate: The primary excitatory neurotransmitter. Dysregulation of metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluRs) has been associated with a subset of ADHD cases, implicating altered synaptic plasticity and cognitive control.
  • Serotonin (5-HT): Although not a primary therapeutic target, serotonergic modulation is under investigation, particularly for addressing comorbid anxiety, mood instability, and impulsivity.

Standard Care & Medications

The standard of care for ADHD is a multimodal approach, combining psychoeducation, behavioural therapy, and pharmacological intervention.

First-Line: Central Nervous System (CNS) Stimulants

Stimulants remain the most effective pharmacological treatment, acting primarily by increasing the synaptic concentration of DA and NE.

Class Examples (Brand Names) Mechanism of Action
Methylphenidates (MPH) Ritalin (Novartis), Concerta (Janssen), Quillivant XR (Tris Pharma) Reuptake Inhibitor: Dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake inhibition via blockade of DAT and NET, increasing neurotransmitter availability in the synaptic cleft.
Amphetamines (AMP) Adderall (Takeda), Vyvanse (Lisdexamfetamine) (Takeda) Block DAT & NET: Enters the presynaptic neuron and inhibits VMAT-2 (Vesicular Monoamine Transporter 2) to promote dopamine release from presynaptic vesicles, redistribute DA to the cytosol, and induce "reverse transport" via the DAT.

Inhibits monoamine oxidase (MAO) activity to prevent the breakdown of dopamine, thereby increasing catecholaminergic efflux.

Safety and Limitations: Common adverse effects include decreased appetite (reported in up to 80% of patients), weight loss, insomnia, and headaches. As Schedule II controlled substances, stimulants carry a risk of diversion and misuse. However, evidence indicates that appropriate therapeutic use in individuals with ADHD does not increase the risk of substance use disorders.

Second-Line: Non-Stimulants

Non-stimulant medications are indicated when stimulants are poorly tolerated, contraindicated, or when 24-hour coverage is necessary. They are critical for the ~20% of patients who are stimulant-refractory.

Strattera (Atomoxetine)
  • Manufacturer: Eli Lilly (FDA approved 2002)
  • Class: Selective SNRI
  • Mechanism: Blocks NET to increase NE and DA levels in the PFC. Because there are few NETs in the nucleus accumbens (reward center), it does not increase dopamine in reward pathways, granting it low abuse potential.
  • Clinical Profile: Delayed onset (2–4 weeks; up to 6 weeks for max efficacy).
Intuniv (Guanfacine)
  • Manufacturer: Takeda (FDA approved 2009)
  • Class: Selective α2A Adrenergic Receptor Agonist
  • Mechanism: Stimulates postsynaptic α2A receptors in the PFC, inhibits cyclic AMP production, and closes HCN channels, thereby strengthening synaptic connectivity and improving working memory.
Kapvay (Clonidine)
  • Manufacturer: Shionogi (FDA approved 2010)
  • Class: Selective α2A Adrenergic Receptor Agonist
  • Clinical Profile: Often used to manage hyperactivity.
Medication Mechanism of Action Notes
Atomoxetine (Strattera) Selective Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitor (SNRI). 2002 FDA approved; Increases DA and NE specifically in the PFC, with minimal effect on the striatum, lowering abuse potential.
Guanfacine (Intuniv) Selective α2A Adrenergic Receptor Agonist 2009 FDA approved; Strengthens PFC function by enhancing NE signalling in key cognitive circuits.
Clonidine (Kapvay) α2A Adrenergic Receptor Agonist (less selective) 2010 FDA approved; Often used for managing hyperactivity, impulsivity, and co-occurring sleep disturbances.
Viloxazine (Qelbree) Selective NET Inhibitor with 5-HT2B/2C receptor activity. Recently approved (2021/2022) for children and adults; offers a non-stimulant option with a unique secondary mechanism.

Novel Medications in the Market: The Next Generation

  • Qelbree (Viloxazine Extended-Release): Supernus (FDA 2021/2022). Class: SNMA. Mechanism: Inhibits NE reuptake in the PFC and modulates serotonin receptors (5-HT2B antagonism, 5-HT2C agonism). Hypothesized to contribute to broad effects on mood and impulsivity. Clinical Profile: Significant symptom reduction as early as Week 1 (pediatric) and Week 2 (adults).
  • Azstarys (Serdexmethylphenidate/Dexmethylphenidate): KemPharm (FDA 2021). Class: Prodrug Stimulant (Schedule II). Mechanism: Fixed-dose combination of IR d-MPH and its prodrug SDX. SDX is pharmacologically inactive until converted by enzymes in the lower GI tract. Clinical Profile: Superiority over placebo from 30 minutes to 13 hours post-dose.

Pipeline Watch: Novel Medications in Clinical Trials

The 2025/2026 pipeline focuses on multi-target agents and advanced delivery systems.

Near Approval: NDA submitted

  • Centanafadine (SNDRI): Otsuka Pharmaceutical. Status: NDA submitted (Nov 2025). Mechanism: First-in-class Serotonin-Norepinephrine-Dopamine Reuptake Inhibitor. Inhibits transporters for all 3 monoamines. Significance: Promising for ADHD with comorbid mood/anxiety.
  • CTx-1301 (Triple-Release Dexmethylphenidate): Cingulate Inc. Status: NDA accepted; PDUFA date May 31, 2026. Mechanism: Proprietary triple-release technology delivering medication in 3 distinct pulses. Significance: Up to 16 hours of control, addressing late-day rebound.

Undergoing Phase 3 Clinical Trials

Research is actively exploring mechanisms beyond the traditional catecholamine targets.

  • Solriamfetol (Sunosi): Axsome Therapeutics. Class: DNRI with TAAR1 and 5-HT1A agonism. Clinical Data: Phase 3 FOCUS Trial (March 2025) showed 45% mean reduction in symptoms. Significance: High efficacy with potentially cleaner CV safety profile.
  • Mazindol ER (NLS-2): NLS Pharmaceutics. Mechanism: Triple monoamine reuptake inhibitor and partial orexin-2 receptor agonist. Significance: Targets "hypo-arousal" via the orexin system.

Conclusion

The field of ADHD pharmacotherapy is undergoing a significant evolution. While the catecholamine hypothesis remains central, the development of multi-target agents like Centanafadine and the exploration of non-catecholaminergic pathways (e.g., glutamate and orexin systems) are creating a personalized treatment landscape. These advancements promise better-tolerated options for the diverse spectrum of individuals affected by ADHD.
