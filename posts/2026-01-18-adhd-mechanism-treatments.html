Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a complex neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere with functioning or development. As of 2025, the understanding of ADHD has evolved from a simplistic “chemical imbalance” model to a sophisticated neurobiological framework involving multiple neurotransmitter systems and neural circuits.

As the most prevalent neurodevelopmental disorder, ADHD affects approximately 7.2% to 11% of children globally. Longitudinal data indicate that ADHD persists into adulthood in approximately 4% to 5% of the general population. Furthermore, emerging studies suggest that up to 20% of adult cases may first manifest clinically significant symptoms during late adolescence, a phenomenon described as “late-onset” ADHD.

Diagnosis and Symptoms

The clinical diagnosis is based on DSM-5 criteria, requiring a persistent pattern of symptoms for at least six months, with an onset before age 12, occurring in more than one setting.

ADHD is phenotypically heterogeneous and is stratified into three primary clinical presentations:

  • Predominantly Inattentive Presentation (ADHD-I): Difficulty sustaining attention, poor organizational skills, and a tendency to abandon tasks. Frequently underdiagnosed due to lack of overt disruption.
  • Predominantly Hyperactive/Impulsive Presentation (ADHD-H): Excessive motor activity, fidgeting, difficulty remaining seated, and impulsive decision-making. Most commonly identified in early childhood.
  • Combined Presentation (ADHD-C): The most prevalent form, meeting criteria for both domains.

Mechanism of Action: Pathophysiology

The core neurobiological dysfunction involves dysregulation of catecholaminergic signalling—primarily dopamine (DA) and norepinephrine (NE)—within executive control networks.

Catecholamine signaling in the Prefrontal Cortex

The Catecholamine Hypothesis

  • Dopamine (DA): Modulates D1 receptor activity to reduce background “noise,” enhancing focus on task-relevant stimuli. Insufficient DA in the prefrontal cortex (PFC) reduces the signal-to-noise ratio, impairing attention.
  • Norepinephrine (NE): Enhances "signal" strength via α2A receptors. Deficiency in the PFC impairs the ability to filter out irrelevant stimuli.

Emerging Neurotransmitter Systems

  • Glutamate: Dysregulation of metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluRs) implies altered synaptic plasticity.
  • Serotonin (5-HT): Under investigation for addressing comorbid anxiety and mood instability.

Standard Care & Medications

The standard of care is multimodal, combining psychoeducation, behavioural therapy, and pharmacotherapy.

First-Line: CNS Stimulants

Stimulants increase synaptic concentrations of DA and NE. They remain the most effective pharmacological intervention.

Class Examples Mechanism of Action
Methylphenidates (MPH) Ritalin, Concerta, Quillivant XR Reuptake Inhibitor: Blockade of DAT and NET, increasing neurotransmitter availability in the synaptic cleft.
Amphetamines (AMP) Adderall, Vyvanse Releaser & Inhibitor: Inhibits VMAT-2 to promote DA release; induces "reverse transport" via DAT; inhibits MAO activity.

Second-Line: Non-Stimulants

Indicated when stimulants are poorly tolerated or contraindicated. These are critical for the ~20% of patients who are stimulant-refractory.

Medication Mechanism Clinical Notes
Strattera (Atomoxetine) Selective NRI (SNRI) Increases DA/NE in PFC with minimal effect on reward pathways (low abuse potential). Delayed onset (2–6 weeks).
Intuniv (Guanfacine) Selective α2A Agonist Strengthens synaptic connectivity in PFC. Particularly effective for emotional dysregulation.
Qelbree (Viloxazine) Selective NET Inhibitor + 5-HT2B/2C activity Newer option (2021). Serotonergic activity may aid mood regulation. Rapid onset (1-2 weeks).

Novel Medications: The Next Generation

Recent approvals have focused on optimizing delivery and broadening receptor targets.

  • Azstarys (Prodrug Stimulant): A combination of immediate-release dexmethylphenidate and its prodrug serdexmethylphenidate. It provides a smooth effect from 30 minutes up to 13 hours.
  • Qelbree (Viloxazine XR): A Serotonin-Norepinephrine Modulating Agent (SNMA) that offers a non-stimulant option with a unique secondary mechanism targeting serotonin receptors.

Pipeline Watch: Clinical Trials (2025/2026)

The pipeline focuses on multi-target agents and advanced delivery systems.

ADHD Drug Development Pipeline 2026

Near Approval (NDA Submitted)

  • Centanafadine (Otsuka): A first-in-class SNDRI (Serotonin-Norepinephrine-Dopamine Reuptake Inhibitor). By inhibiting transporters for all three monoamines, it leverages focus-enhancing effects while harnessing mood-stabilizing properties. NDA submitted Nov 2025.
  • CTx-1301 (Cingulate): Uses triple-release technology to deliver medication in 3 distinct pulses, targeting 16-hour symptom control. PDUFA date: May 31, 2026.

Phase 3 Candidates

Candidate Mechanism Significance
Solriamfetol DNRI + TAAR1 Agonist Potential non-scheduled alternative with a cleaner cardiovascular profile than amphetamines.
Mazindol ER Triple Reuptake + Orexin-2 Agonist Targets the "hypo-arousal" characteristic of inattention via the orexin wakefulness system.
Fasoracetam mGluR Activator Precision medicine approach for patients with specific mGluR gene mutations.

Conclusion

The field of ADHD pharmacotherapy is undergoing a significant evolution. While the catecholamine hypothesis remains central, the development of multi-target agents like Centanafadine and the exploration of non-catecholaminergic pathways (e.g., glutamate and orexin systems) are creating a personalized treatment landscape.
