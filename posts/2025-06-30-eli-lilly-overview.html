Eli Lilly and Company (LLY), established in 1876, is a centennial global pharmaceutical powerhouse dedicated to developing and marketing medicines and vaccines across various therapeutic areas, including Alzheimer's disease, diabetes, oncology, metabolic disorders, and autoimmune diseases.

The company boasts a diversified product portfolio featuring market-leading drugs such as Trulicity (dulaglutide) for diabetes, Verzenio (abemaciclib) for breast cancer, and Taltz for psoriasis. However, its current growth is primarily driven by its blockbuster GLP-1 receptor agonists: Mounjaro (tirzepatide) and Zepbound (tirzepatide).

Lilly's current market valuation reflects strong investor confidence, largely due to its competitive advantage in the weight loss and type 2 diabetes markets. While the entry of competitors beyond Novo Nordisk poses a challenge to maintaining market share over the next three years, Lilly is aggressively expanding its pipeline beyond weight management.

History and Key Milestones

Eli Lilly was founded in 1876 by Colonel Eli Lilly, a pharmaceutical chemist and Civil War veteran. After serving in the Union Army (1861–1865), he worked in several pharmacies before establishing his own manufacturing business.

Early Development and Innovation (1876 – 1940s)

  • 1876: Colonel Eli Lilly founded the company after a brief partnership with John F. Johnston. The initial focus was on high-quality drugs, including quinine for malaria.
  • 1880s: Introduced innovations such as gelatin-coated pills/capsules and fruit-flavoured or sugar-coated tablets for easier ingestion.
  • 1923: Commercialization of Insulin. In collaboration with the University of Toronto, Lilly became the first company to mass-produce insulin (Iletin), establishing its leadership in diabetes care.
  • 1940s: Pioneered the mass production of penicillin for Allied forces during World War II.

Post-War Expansion (1950s – 1980s)

  • 1952: Became a publicly traded company.
  • 1954: Entered animal health by establishing Elanco Products Company.
  • 1955: Became one of the first manufacturers of the Salk polio vaccine.
  • 1961: Developed its first oncology drug.
  • 1971: Acquired Elizabeth Arden (cosmetics), later sold in 1987.
  • 1977: Expanded into medical devices by acquiring IVAC and Cardiac Pacemakers.

Biotech Rise and Neuroscience Breakthroughs (1990s – Present)

  • 1982: Launched Humulin® (human insulin), becoming one of the first companies to use recombinant DNA technology, cementing its diabetes leadership.
  • 1986/1987: Launched Prozac® (fluoxetine). This selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) revolutionized depression treatment and established Lilly as a major player in neuroscience.

Modern Challenges and Transformation (2000s – Present)

  • 2011: Formed an alliance with Boehringer Ingelheim to develop diabetes drugs, including Jardiance®.
  • 2014: Acquired Novartis’ animal health business to strengthen Elanco (fully spun off in 2019).
  • 2020: Rapidly developed monoclonal antibody therapies in response to the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • 2022 – 2025: Executed a series of strategic acquisitions, including Akouos (gene therapy for hearing loss), DICE Therapeutics (oral immunology), Sigilon Therapeutics (cell therapy for diabetes), Versanis Bio (cardiometabolic disease), Mablink Biosciences (ADCs), POINT Biopharma (radioligand therapy), Morphic Therapeutic (oral integrin therapies), and Scorpion Therapeutics' PI3Kα inhibitor program (precision oncology).
  • 2024: Kisunla™ (donanemab) approved in the U.S. for early Alzheimer's disease; Ebglyss® (lebrikizumab) approved for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis.
  • 2025: Continued focus on acquisitions and manufacturing capacity expansion.

Pharmaceutical Product Portfolio

Eli Lilly maintains a robust pipeline addressing significant unmet medical needs.

Top-Selling Products

  • Mounjaro (tirzepatide): A dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist for type 2 diabetes. In 2024, it generated $11.54 billion in sales. Analysts project it could become the world's best-selling drug by 2030, with estimated sales of $36 billion, driven by its superior efficacy in glycemic control and weight reduction.
  • Zepbound (tirzepatide): Sharing the same active ingredient as Mounjaro but indicated for weight management. In 2024, it also received FDA approval for Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA). Despite launching in late 2023, it rapidly accumulated $4.93 billion in 2024 sales. Evaluate Pharma predicts Zepbound will be the world’s third best-selling drug by 2030 ($25.5 billion).
  • Trulicity (dulaglutide): A GLP-1 receptor agonist and a cornerstone of Lilly’s diabetes portfolio. Despite patent expiration pressures and competition, it generated approximately $7.1 billion in 2024. Its patent expires in 2027, after which generic competition may erode sales.
  • Verzenio (abemaciclib): A CDK4/6 inhibitor for HR-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer. It showed strong growth with $5.31 billion in 2024 sales.
  • Jardiance (empagliflozin): An SGLT2 inhibitor co-developed with Boehringer Ingelheim for type 2 diabetes, heart failure, and CKD. 2024 revenue reached $3.34 billion, a 7.4% year-over-year increase.

Recent Approvals

  • Kisunla™ (donanemab-azbt): An anti-amyloid beta (Aβ) monoclonal antibody approved by the FDA in July 2024 for early symptomatic Alzheimer's disease.
  • Tirzepatide (New Indication): In the Phase 3 SUMMIT trial for patients with Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF) and obesity, Tirzepatide reduced heart failure outcomes by 38% and hospitalization risk by 56%. Lilly has submitted applications to the FDA and EMA, potentially expanding its market into cardiovascular disease.
  • Omvoh (mirikizumab): An IL-23 antagonist approved in 2023 for ulcerative colitis and in January 2024 for Crohn’s disease.
  • EBGLYSS (lebrikizumab): An IL-13 antagonist approved in September 2024 for atopic dermatitis. Phase 3 trials are ongoing for chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps (CRSwNP) and perennial allergic rhinitis.

Phase 3 Candidates with High Potential

  • Orforglipron: An oral GLP-1 receptor agonist. The Phase 3 ACHIEVE-1 trial demonstrated significant efficacy in HbA1c and weight reduction with a safety profile consistent with injectable GLP-1s. Lilly expects to file for FDA approval for type 2 diabetes in 2026. As the first oral GLP-1 to complete Phase 3, it holds immense market potential due to convenience.
  • Remternetug: A next-generation anti-Aβ monoclonal antibody for Alzheimer's, currently in Phase 3.

Early-Stage Candidates

  • Lepodisiran: A siRNA therapy targeting the LPA gene to degrade apo(a) mRNA. Phase 2 data showed high doses reduced Lp(a) by 94% after 180 days and maintained a 91% reduction at one year (74.2% at 1.5 years), suggesting infrequent dosing potential.
  • Muvalaplin: An oral small molecule that disrupts the apo(a)-apo B100 interaction to inhibit Lp(a) formation. Phase 2 results showed up to 85.8% reduction in Lp(a) after 12 weeks, with 96.7% of patients achieving target levels.

Patent Expirations (Loss of Exclusivity)

  • Trulicity (dulaglutide): Expires 2027. While generic competition is expected, the impact may be mitigated by the uptake of Tirzepatide.
  • Cyramza (ramucirumab): Expires 2026. 2024 sales were $973.3 million (2.2% growth).
  • Taltz (ixekizumab): Biologics data protection expires in 2028; compound patent expires in 2030. A key immunology asset (IL-17A antagonist) with 2024 sales of $3.26 billion.

Note: Biologics Data Protection (12 years in the US) prevents competitors from using the originator's data for biosimilar applications. Compound Patents (20 years) prevent the manufacture/sale of the specific molecule.

Strategic Acquisitions and M&A

Lilly utilizes a "string-of-pearls" or "bolt-on" acquisition strategy to access cutting-edge technologies and bolster its pipeline in oncology, immunology, neuroscience, and metabolic diseases.

Key Acquisitions in First Half of 2025:

  • Scorpion Therapeutics (STX-478): Acquired in Q1 2025 to expand precision oncology. STX-478 is a mutant-selective PI3Kα inhibitor potentially suitable for 30-40% of HR+ breast cancer patients, designed to spare healthy tissue. The deal is valued at up to $2.5 billion.
  • SiteOne Therapeutics (STC-004): Acquired for up to $1 billion. STC-004 is a non-opioid pain reliever targeting the Nav1.8 sodium channel (Phase 2 ready). This diversifies Lilly's pipeline into non-addictive pain management, competing directly with Vertex Pharma’s recently approved Journavx.
  • Verve Therapeutics (VERVE-102): Acquired for up to $1.3 billion to leverage gene editing for cardiovascular disease. VERVE-102 is a base-editing therapy designed to permanently lower PCSK9 for ASCVD treatment. Lilly exercised its opt-in right via a full acquisition.

Competitive Landscape

Obesity & Diabetes

Primary Competitor: Novo Nordisk (Semaglutide: Wegovy/Ozempic/Rybelsus).
Comparison: Recent clinical data indicates Zepbound (tirzepatide) may offer superior weight loss efficacy compared to Wegovy. However, Novo Nordisk has an oral semaglutide (Rybelsus) already approved for diabetes and in Phase 3 for obesity.

Oncology (PI3Kα Inhibitors)

Primary Competitor: Novartis (Alpelisib/PIQRAY).
Differentiation: Lilly's STX-478 is mutant-selective, potentially avoiding metabolic toxicities (e.g., hyperglycemia) associated with non-selective inhibitors like Alpelisib. Furthermore, STX-478 is a monotherapy candidate, whereas Alpelisib requires combination with fulvestrant injections.

Cholesterol / Lp(a)

SiRNA Competitors: Amgen (Olpasiran) and Silence Therapeutics (Zerlasiran) show strong efficacy (>80-95% reduction). Lilly's Lepodisiran differentiates via its long duration of action (up to 1.5 years).
PCSK9 Competitors: Novartis (Leqvio), Amgen (Repatha), Regeneron (Praluent), and Merck (MK-0616 oral macrocyclic peptide). While the PCSK9 market is crowded, Lilly’s Lp(a) therapies address a distinct risk factor and could be complementary.

Atopic Dermatitis

Competitors: Sanofi/Regeneron (Dupixent) and LEO Pharma (Adbry).
Analysis: Like its competitors, EBGLYSS is an injectable antibody (bi-weekly). It faces stiff competition from oral JAK inhibitors like Pfizer's Cibinqo and Lilly's own Olumiant (baricitinib), as well as topical options like Incyte's Opzelura.

Summary

Eli Lilly and Company remains a dominant force in the pharmaceutical industry, anchored by its historical leadership in diabetes and its current dominance with Mounjaro and Zepbound. With 2024 revenue growing 32% and net income doubling, the company is aggressively reinvesting in manufacturing and R&D.

Despite looming patent cliffs for legacy drugs like Trulicity, Lilly is successfully diversifying through strategic bolt-on acquisitions in oncology, gene editing, and immunology. The advancement of high-potential assets like the oral GLP-1 Orforglipron and the long-acting Lepodisiran positions Lilly to sustain its growth momentum and leadership in medical innovation for the coming decade.
